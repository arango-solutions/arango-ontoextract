# Global Bank Financial Crime Investigation Casebook

> Synthetic demonstration document for AOE ontology extraction. All organizations,
> people, account numbers, transaction references, and events are fictional.

## Executive Summary

Northbridge Global Bank operates a multi-jurisdiction financial crime program
covering customer due diligence, sanctions screening, transaction monitoring,
fraud analytics, correspondent banking risk, and suspicious activity reporting.
This casebook describes a complex investigation spanning retail accounts,
corporate entities, trade finance instruments, shell-company networks, crypto
off-ramps, and mule account activity.

The demonstration scenario is designed to expose many ontology classes and
relations: customers hold accounts, accounts originate transactions, alerts are
generated by scenarios, analysts review evidence, cases contain alerts,
investigations produce findings, and regulatory reports are filed with competent
authorities.

## Institution Profile

Northbridge Global Bank is a fictional universal bank with operations in the
United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, Singapore, Germany, and Mexico. The
bank serves individual customers, small businesses, multinational corporates,
money services businesses, charities, fintech platforms, and correspondent
banks.

The bank maintains the following operating divisions:

- Retail Banking, which manages checking accounts, savings accounts, consumer
  cards, residential mortgages, digital wallets, and personal loans.
- Commercial Banking, which manages treasury services, deposit accounts,
  commercial credit facilities, merchant acquiring, and payroll services.
- Corporate and Investment Banking, which manages trade finance, securities
  custody, structured lending, foreign exchange, and capital markets products.
- Correspondent Banking, which maintains nostro accounts, vostro accounts,
  payable-through accounts, SWIFT access, and nested banking relationships.
- Financial Crime Compliance, which owns AML, sanctions, fraud, anti-bribery,
  anti-corruption, tax evasion controls, and regulatory reporting.

## Core Entities

### Customer

A Customer is a person or organization with a formal relationship to the bank.
Each customer has a customer identifier, onboarding date, risk rating, segment,
jurisdiction, residency status, source of wealth, source of funds, expected
activity profile, and relationship manager.

Individual customers include natural persons such as deposit account holders,
credit card holders, beneficial owners, signatories, directors, trustees,
protectors, and power-of-attorney delegates.

Corporate customers include limited liability companies, partnerships, trusts,
foundations, charities, investment funds, foreign financial institutions, and
special purpose vehicles.

### Account

An Account is a ledger relationship maintained by the bank. Account types
include demand deposit account, savings account, prepaid account, merchant
settlement account, escrow account, client money account, correspondent account,
nostro account, vostro account, margin account, and collateral account.

Each account records an account number, opening date, status, currency, branch,
relationship manager, product type, balance, average daily balance, authorized
signatories, and linked customer relationships.

### Transaction

A Transaction is a movement of value. Transaction channels include ACH, wire,
SWIFT, SEPA, FPS, card purchase, ATM withdrawal, check deposit, remote deposit
capture, internal transfer, foreign exchange trade, trade finance drawdown,
merchant settlement, and crypto exchange payout.

Each transaction has an originator, beneficiary, amount, currency, timestamp,
channel, counterparty institution, reference text, narrative, purpose code,
device identifier, IP address, geolocation, and disposition.

### Alert

An Alert is generated by a monitoring scenario or fraud rule. Alerts have alert
identifiers, trigger dates, scenario codes, severity, score, threshold, linked
transactions, linked accounts, and current status.

Common statuses are new, assigned, in review, escalated, closed false positive,
closed with finding, quality review, and reopened.

### Case

A Case groups alerts, customers, transactions, documents, investigative notes,
evidence files, and decisions. Each case has a case owner, priority, due date,
case type, suspicion category, narrative, disposition, and regulatory deadline.

## Customer Network

### Primary Corporate Customer: Meridian Import Group LLC

Meridian Import Group LLC is a Delaware limited liability company onboarded by
the Commercial Banking division. Meridian states that it imports consumer
electronics, replacement parts, and industrial sensors from suppliers in East
Asia and resells them to wholesalers in North America.

Meridian has the following attributes:

- Legal entity identifier: MER-DEL-948201.
- Incorporation jurisdiction: Delaware.
- Principal business address: Wilmington, Delaware.
- Operating address: Port Newark warehouse district.
- Stated annual revenue: USD 48,000,000.
- Employee count: 37.
- Industry code: wholesale electronics distributor.
- Relationship manager: Priya N.
- Initial risk rating: medium.
- Current risk rating: high.

Meridian owns three operating accounts: a USD operating account, a EUR
collection account, and a merchant settlement account. The company also has a
revolving credit facility and a standby letter of credit line.

### Beneficial Ownership

Meridian disclosed two ultimate beneficial owners:

- Daniel Hart, holding 60 percent ownership through Hart Family Holdings LLC.
- Lucia Varga, holding 40 percent ownership through Varga Trading Trust.

Daniel Hart is also a director of Atlas Components Ltd., a United Kingdom
company that receives payments from Meridian. Lucia Varga is a signatory on
Meridian accounts and a beneficial owner of Blue Harbor Logistics Pte Ltd. in
Singapore.

### Related Counterparties

The investigation identifies several related counterparties:

- Atlas Components Ltd., a UK distributor that invoices Meridian for sensor
  assemblies.
- Blue Harbor Logistics Pte Ltd., a Singapore freight forwarder that receives
  logistics payments from Meridian.
- Cobalt Digital Exchange, a virtual asset service provider used by several
  individual customers in the network.
- North River Consulting SA, a Panama entity receiving consulting fees.
- Twin Pines Charitable Foundation, a charity with incoming wires from Meridian
  and outgoing transfers to unrelated individuals.
- RQ Metals FZE, a UAE free-zone entity appearing on several trade invoices.

## Account Relationships

The USD operating account for Meridian receives merchant settlements, incoming
domestic wires, and foreign currency conversions. It sends outgoing wires to
foreign suppliers, logistics providers, consultants, and affiliated entities.

The EUR collection account receives SEPA transfers from European buyers and
converts balances to USD through foreign exchange trades. The account also sends
periodic round-number wires to Atlas Components Ltd.

The merchant settlement account receives card settlement credits from two
merchant acquirers. Several batches include unusual refund activity, high charge
back ratios, and late-night settlement reversals.

Daniel Hart holds a personal premium checking account, a brokerage account, and
a private client credit card. Lucia Varga holds a personal checking account, a
safe deposit box, and a home equity line of credit.

## Transaction Typologies

### Rapid Movement of Funds

Rapid movement occurs when incoming wires are followed by outgoing wires within
one business day. Meridian received USD 1,250,000 from Westlake Wholesale Inc.
and sent USD 1,220,000 to RQ Metals FZE on the same day. The transaction memo
referenced "inventory deposit", but no purchase order was attached.

### Round-Dollar International Wires

Meridian initiated repeated round-dollar payments of USD 100,000, USD 250,000,
and USD 500,000 to North River Consulting SA. The consulting invoices used
generic descriptions such as "regional advisory services" and "market access
support" without deliverables, hourly rates, or named consultants.

### Trade-Based Money Laundering Indicators

Trade documents showed inconsistent product descriptions. One invoice described
"industrial sensors", while the shipping manifest described "plastic housings".
The declared customs value was substantially higher than market benchmarks.
The bill of lading listed Blue Harbor Logistics as freight forwarder, Meridian
as consignee, and RQ Metals FZE as shipper.

### Funnel Account Activity

Five individual accounts received cash deposits at branches in different states.
The deposits were made below internal cash-reporting thresholds and were
followed by Zelle transfers to Daniel Hart and card purchases at electronics
retailers. The individuals had no apparent relationship to Meridian during
onboarding.

### Crypto Off-Ramp Pattern

Cobalt Digital Exchange sent ACH credits to three Northbridge customers. Within
hours, those customers purchased cashier checks payable to Twin Pines Charitable
Foundation. The foundation then wired funds to North River Consulting SA.

## Monitoring Scenarios

### Scenario AML-101: Structuring

The structuring scenario identifies repeated cash deposits below USD 10,000
within a rolling seven-day period. It aggregates deposits by customer, account,
branch, device, cash-in ticket, and depositor identity.

Alert AML-101-8871 triggered for customer Ethan Gray after six cash deposits
totaling USD 54,800 across four branches. Ethan Gray later sent funds to Daniel
Hart.

### Scenario AML-207: Rapid Movement

The rapid movement scenario flags incoming funds followed by outbound transfers
that consume at least 80 percent of incoming value within 48 hours. It excludes
known payroll accounts and documented sweep accounts.

Alert AML-207-4493 triggered for Meridian after a same-day pass-through pattern.

### Scenario AML-330: Trade Finance Anomaly

The trade finance scenario compares invoice values, product descriptions,
country risk, vessel routing, and customer expected activity. It flags
over-invoicing, under-invoicing, phantom shipments, dual-use goods, inconsistent
HS codes, and sanctioned vessel exposure.

Alert AML-330-1022 linked three invoices, two bills of lading, one letter of
credit amendment, and a customs entry summary.

### Scenario SAN-041: Sanctions Name Match

The sanctions screening scenario checks customers, counterparties, vessels,
ports, beneficial owners, and narrative text against watchlists. It records
match strength, list source, alias, date of birth, jurisdiction, and disposition.

RQ Metals FZE generated a weak fuzzy match against a sanctioned entity alias.
The screening team closed the match as not the listed party but required
enhanced due diligence because the counterparty operated in a high-risk free
zone.

### Scenario FRD-512: Merchant Refund Abuse

The merchant fraud scenario monitors refund ratios, chargeback codes, merchant
category codes, terminal identifiers, card-present indicators, and device
fingerprints. Meridian's merchant settlement account showed a refund ratio of
18 percent against a peer average of 3 percent.

## Investigation Timeline

### Day 1: Alert Triage

Analyst Maya Chen received three alerts: AML-207-4493, AML-330-1022, and
FRD-512-7780. She reviewed customer profiles, expected activity, prior alerts,
transaction history, KYC documents, merchant statements, and trade finance
files.

Maya linked the alerts into Case FC-2026-0418 because the same corporate
customer, related counterparties, and trade documents appeared across multiple
scenarios.

### Day 3: Enhanced Due Diligence Request

The analyst requested updated beneficial ownership documentation, supplier
contracts, proof of shipment, warehouse receipts, inspection certificates, and
consulting deliverables. Meridian provided some invoices but did not provide
complete shipping records for RQ Metals FZE shipments.

### Day 7: Relationship Manager Interview

The relationship manager stated that Meridian had recently expanded into
industrial sensors and relied on Blue Harbor Logistics for customs clearance.
The relationship manager had not visited Meridian's warehouse and had not met
North River Consulting SA representatives.

### Day 10: Network Analysis

The financial intelligence unit built a network graph linking customers,
accounts, counterparties, transactions, IP addresses, device identifiers,
addresses, phone numbers, and beneficial owners. Shared identifiers connected
Meridian, Atlas Components Ltd., Blue Harbor Logistics, and Daniel Hart.

### Day 14: Escalation

The case was escalated to the suspicious activity reporting team. The suspicion
categories were trade-based money laundering, funnel account activity,
potential sanctions evasion, and unexplained third-party payments.

## Findings

The investigation found that Meridian's stated business activity did not fully
explain transaction volumes, counterparty selection, invoice values, or cash
deposit links. The beneficial owners had overlapping roles across several
counterparties, but those relationships were not disclosed at onboarding.

The analyst concluded that there was a reasonable basis to suspect the movement
of funds through Meridian and related parties may involve trade-based money
laundering and the use of third-party mule accounts.

## Regulatory Reporting

The suspicious activity reporting team drafted a Suspicious Activity Report.
The SAR narrative described the customer, account relationships, transaction
patterns, trade documentation anomalies, related counterparties, and reasons
for suspicion. The filing deadline was 30 calendar days from detection.

The report was filed with the financial intelligence unit in the relevant
jurisdiction. The bank also placed the customer relationship under exit review
and increased monitoring thresholds pending closure.

## Controls and Governance

The case identified control gaps:

- Customer due diligence did not capture all affiliated entities.
- Relationship manager site visit requirements were not enforced.
- Trade finance document review did not compare product descriptions across
  invoice, bill of lading, and customs documents.
- Merchant refund monitoring was not linked to AML monitoring until escalation.
- Crypto exchange payout monitoring did not automatically connect related
  cashier check purchases.

Remediation actions include scenario tuning, beneficial ownership refresh,
trade document analytics, enhanced merchant monitoring, and improved case
linkage rules.
